This is a Carphone

Exactly what it sounds like, a Carphone is a phone for your car. The idea of a dedicated phone for your car may seem strange nowadays, but they were once seen as a great innovation, and even a status symbol. Carphone's started becoming mainstream around the 1970's, remained popular during the 1980's, and started to die in the 1990's as personal cell phones became more popular.

Carphone's during that time required much more power to function compared to cellphones of today, that power requirement was sated by a car battery. Also, due to the cell signal infrastructure of the time, both long antenna's and the great outdoors were requirements for making quality mobile phone calls.

But what about before cell technology?

Before cell service infrastructure, Carphone's used radio frequencies to make calls. However this was quite expensive, as each Carphone would be assigned its own frequency, meaning there could be only few cardphones in close proximity to function properly. In addition, those signals were not in any way private. This lead to early car phones being seen as a status symbol, and usually were only installed in high end cars.

The Carphone was the answer to a need. The need to make and receive phone calls while out and about, away from your house. Because before, phones were in a stationary place, connected by a wire, to a home. People are mobile creatures, we go out and about, move around, visit places and people regularly, and a stationary telephone is contrary to that lifestyle, and created the need for the Carphone.

The 1980s can be referred to as the golden age of the car phone, as the technology would be more affordable and accessible than the cardphones of the 1970's which saw limited availability and high prices, and still having an advantage over the upcoming hand portable phones of the 1990s that would push the Carphone out of popularity. The 1980s Carphone would be a must have for a business man of the era.

Martin and his phones

Of course, the Carphone still had mobile limitations, the phone is still wired to the car. Those limitations are what pushed Martin Cooper, a Motorola researcher and executive to develop the first truly mobile phone, making the first phone call from a hand-held phone call in 1973. The phone weighed two and a half pounds was 10 inches long, and had a 20 minute battery life.
That first hand-help phone call made by Martin Cooper was made to one of his competitors, in which he told them he was calling from a completely mobile, hand held phone. This would start a fierce research and development race and rivalry, which would power the swift development, and innovation of hand-held cellphone technology, and eventually lead to phones of today.

Martin Cooper is the car phone killer

The 1990's saw the eventual downfall of the Carphone, as hand held phones, due to advancements in technology, overcame the limitations that held them behind car phones. Sporting a respectable battery life, and comparable call quality, the truly mobile phone of the 90s would finally push ahead of the Carphone.
From a present day perspective, we can see the Carphone as a stepping stone in cellphone technology. The Carphone pushed the telephone out of the home, and would take the telephone industry towards a mobile mindset, which would eventually lead to the smartphones we all carry with us present day. An important step in phone development.
The Carphone is a good example of how innovation builds on what we already have. Mobile phones where an innovation inspired by the car phone, the Carphone was built on the back of existing telephone technology, and telephone technology was an innovation in communication that improved upon the sending of letters.
